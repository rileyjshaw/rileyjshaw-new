  • making nothing

    2018.03.29

    here’s a post that i wrote during my second week here. now that the showcase is coming up, i need to start making a thing. guess i’d better put this up before that starts :)

    one of my major goals during this program is to not make things.

    * silence. the sfpc showcase curators back away slowly to discuss what a mistake they’ve made. *

    it’s okay, i’ll do the assignments and make a showcase project and all that. i’m still going to make things. it’s hard not to; i’ve already made a lot of Things since arriving, more than i’m comfortable with. but i’m celebrating all the things i think of that i don’t make.

    during the binary numbers assignment, i came up with an idea to:

    • map binary digits (bits) to piano keys
    • find binary numbers that resolve to nice chords
    • create a simple matching app that prompts players with a decimal number and celebrates when the correct chord is played

    i have a midi keyboard here, and i’ve written a few programs already that interface with it. throwing this sketch together would have been easy. tbh it would have been an easy way to show off in class too. but just by thinking it through, i’d already gotten as much out of it as i was ever going to. building it would have come from a compulsion, not from any actual question or need. and it would have taken time, and space. for what?

    there’s a passage in Madness, Rack, and Honey that i love:

    if you have any idea for a poem, an exact grid of intent, you are on the wrong path, a dead-end alley, at the top of a cliff you haven’t even climbed.

    moving to new york – even from a big city like toronto – i’m struck by the overabundance of stuff here. stuff to buy. stuff to eat. stuff to stuff your apartment with. it’s a good reminder that less can be more.

  • My School for Poetic Computation application

    2018.03.02

    I’ve decided to post my response to the three questions SFPC asks potential participants.

    Before I start any application process I try to learn as much as I can about the place. That involves learning about the folx involved. I hope this post helps a future SFPC attendee get started on their application.

    If you’ve landed here from a search and are considering applying, I recommend doing so! It’s fun here and the people are nice.

    A disclaimer before we get into it: everyone who goes through SFPC comes from different backgrounds. If you see something on my application that you haven’t experienced, please don’t take it as a requirement. Batches are carefully selected to include different perspectives and skillsets. And you’re awesome 💕

    Why do you want to study at SFPC at this point in your life?

    I love making art! But when I use computers, I can get distracted by Technical Shinys. I also get distracted by Expectations, especially around my Engineering Career.

    In 2014 I was working at a startup without a purpose whatsoever. I left to join Khan Academy, a free education nonprofit. I felt good about that.

    A few years have gone by and I feel a similar pull. I’ve lost my idealism for nonprofits; they’re subject to the same restrictive evaluation metrics and organizational patterns as any corporation.

    (Dang, that sucks.)

    I see art as a way to effect change outside of the rigid hierarchical structures of Work. It’s a chance to connect on a different level; to surprise, engage, and hopefully, to shine light on something better.

    I’d like to recalibrate… again. I wrote a little poem called “whoami” below. I want to work with more expressive languages.

    ——————————————————————————-

    #!/usr/bin/env python
    from subprocess import Popen as ask, PIPE as rain; for_truth_in = bool

    id  = ‘my’ in'stinctual impulse’
    for all_my_be in'g’:
       there = 'more to’.find(“) - (id is not 'critical’ or 'moralizing’)
    superego = there

    ego = i = ask('whoami’, stdout=rain)
    for days in ['the rain’]: i = i.stdout

    reflection = (word for word in [
       'oh, the places you'll go’ in 'the summer’,
       'the art of electronics’ in 'uni’,
       'the lean startup’ in 'my dark days’,
       'the one room schoolhouse’ in 'that idealistic edtech phase’,
       ”,
       ’’,
       ’’,
       'and so on.’
    ])

    found = not 'yet’

    while not found:
       found = i.read(for_truth_in(next(reflection)))

    i = found + ego.communicate()[id][:superego]
    print i

    What do you want the School for Poetic Computation to be?

    APPROACHING IDEAS WITH THOUGHTFULNESS
    =====================================

    I’m familiar with some of the technical methods we’ll cover over the term. This is exciting; re-approaching them intentionally through an artistic lens might help me break forgotten assumptions. So rather than focusing on *how* to build a Markov chain, I want to explore *why* I’d build one.

    I went to Recurse Center in 2014 because I wanted to collaborate with people outside of rigid project structures. I want to explore and build new frameworks now. I hope SFPC can help me rebuild my conceptions around the words “technical”, “art”, and “project”.

    A STUDY IN EDUCATION
    ====================

    I’ve spent the past few years learning about education. I’ve formed opinions and have even more questions now. I’m interested in programs like SFPC that challenge the rigidity of institutional CS or Fine Arts programs.

    I also love mentorship. I lead our formal mentorship program at Khan Academy, and teach as a volunteer at a few local organizations. I’m sure I’ll learn more from my cohort than they learn from me, but when it’s helpful I’d like to pitch in.

    ULTIMATELY,
    ===========

    I’m excited to develop my artistic practice around folks that I’ve admired for a really, really long time <3

    What would you like to make at SFPC?

    As you can see on rileyjshaw.com/lab, I’ve made many little things. These days, I try to work slower. Rather than approaching SFPC with plans to create specific artifacts, I’m more interested in the process. I would like to refine the way that I absorb and synthesize ideas. I want to listen a lot and make slow, deliberate progress toward this goal.

    Here are some topics that I’m interested in:

    EDUCATION AND THE REINFORCEMENT OF CLASS STRUCTURES
    ===================================================

    Our formal education system creates adults who can’t take care of themselves, and whose survival depends on capitalist class structures. Educated and non-educated citizens are trapped in this cycle. A post-class society needs modes of education that break these rigidly imposed hierarchies. What might that look like?

    DELETION, PERMANENCE, AND CONSISTENCY
    =====================================

    Silicon Valley likes to store data because it entrenches people in their past behaviors. Corporate culture’s emphasis on consistency is ableist, colonizing, transphobic, and represents a growing discomfort with people’s fluidity.

    I hope someday to have an International Day of Deletion. How can we live more presently?

    CORPORATE TOOLS
    ===============

    A Craigslist bot gives Sol LeWitt-style instructions to a community. A series of banner ads discourage consumerism. Challenging people’s expectations around advertising and corporate tools may raise questions about the structures behind them.

    Mechanical Turk, Google Ads, Ebay, promoted tweets… these tools enforce the goals and values of capitalism. Can they be used to break them?

    MAKING FRIENDS!!!
    =================

    I am especially excited for the chance to meet new people that I can work with deeply. Festivals like XOXO or Eyeo allow only fleeting glimpses of fellow weirdos. Collaborating and growing together as a cohort – supporting and challenging one-another – is what I’m most excited about.

    In every education program I’ve been a part of so far, people have been the most important part.

    Well, that’s it. I was initially accepted into the Fall 2017 batch but deferred to finish some important work at Khan Academy and transition stewardship of the programs I was looking after. In the past half-year I’ve learned a lot and changed a lot, and my application would look different if I were to write it today. But the heart of this – writing less code, more thoughtfully – is at least as true today as it was when I wrote this.
